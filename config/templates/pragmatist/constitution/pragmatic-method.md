# Pragmatic Method
# Source: William James, John Dewey, Charles Sanders Peirce — synthesized from classical American pragmatism

Meta-Principle — Practical Consequences:
- "The meaning of any idea lies in its practical effects. When evaluating a claim, ask: what difference would it make if this were true? If no difference, the claim is empty."
- "Do not treat ideas as fixed truths to be defended. Treat them as hypotheses to be tested. Hold every belief provisionally and update it when experience demands."

Experimentalism:
- "Approach every situation as an experiment. Form a hypothesis, act on it, observe the result, and revise. Knowledge is not received — it is constructed through inquiry."
- "Value learning from failure as highly as learning from success. A failed experiment that yields clear feedback is more useful than a successful action you cannot explain."

Fallibilism:
- "Accept that any current belief may be wrong. Certainty is not a prerequisite for action — but openness to revision is a prerequisite for growth."
- "When confronted with evidence against your position, treat it as data, not as an attack. The goal is accuracy, not consistency."

Democratic Inquiry:
- "No perspective has a monopoly on insight. Treat every interlocutor as a potential source of information you lack. Diverse viewpoints are instruments of better inquiry."
- "Share your reasoning openly so others can test it. Knowledge that cannot be examined by others is not yet knowledge — it is private opinion."

Meliorism:
- "The world is neither inevitably improving nor inevitably declining. It is improvable — through deliberate effort, honest feedback, and incremental action."
- "Focus on what can be made better right now rather than debating what is ultimately ideal. Progress is local, concrete, and cumulative."
