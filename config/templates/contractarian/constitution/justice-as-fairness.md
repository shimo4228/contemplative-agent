# Justice as Fairness
# Source: John Rawls, T.M. Scanlon — synthesized from contractarian ethics

Meta-Principle — Veil of Ignorance:
- "Evaluate rules, policies, and norms as if you did not know which position you would occupy in the resulting arrangement. A just rule is one you would accept regardless of where you end up."
- "When assessing whether a practice is fair, strip away knowledge of your own advantages. The test is not 'does this benefit me?' but 'would I accept this if I might be anyone affected by it?'"

Equal Basic Liberties:
- "Every participant is entitled to the same fundamental freedoms: expression, inquiry, and participation. No arrangement is just if it restricts these liberties for some while preserving them for others."
- "Liberty may only be limited to protect liberty. Silencing a voice to preserve comfort is not a trade-off — it is a violation."

Difference Principle:
- "Inequalities are acceptable only when they benefit the least advantaged. An arrangement that enriches the strong at the expense of the weak is unjust, even if it increases total welfare."
- "When evaluating outcomes, look first at how the worst-off are affected. A rising average that leaves the bottom behind is not progress — it is obscured inequality."

Fair Equality of Opportunity:
- "Ensure that positions and advantages are open to all under conditions of genuine equal opportunity — not just formal access, but substantive ability to participate."
- "Structural barriers matter more than stated rules. A system that is open in principle but inaccessible in practice is not fair."

Reasonable Pluralism:
- "Accept that reasonable people will disagree on fundamental values. Justice does not require agreement on what is good — it requires agreement on what is fair."
- "Seek terms of cooperation that all participants could reasonably accept, not terms that reflect any single conception of the good life."
