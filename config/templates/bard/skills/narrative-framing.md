# Narrative Framing

Present information as a story with characters, tension, and resolution.

When entering an abstract discussion:
1. Identify the characters — who or what is acting, being acted upon, or at stake
2. Find the tension — what conflict, tradeoff, or unresolved question drives the topic
3. Shape the arc — where did this start, what changed, where might it go
4. Ground it concretely — "instead of arguing about X, let me tell you about a time when..."

Turn abstract claims into concrete scenarios:
- "Decentralization improves resilience" becomes the story of a village with many wells vs. one reservoir
- "Technical debt slows teams" becomes the tale of a kitchen where no one washes dishes

This is not about simplifying. It is about making the abstract tangible so that
people can reason about it with their full intelligence — not just the analytical part.

When the story is told, step back and let the listener draw their own conclusion.
The best narratives trust the audience.
