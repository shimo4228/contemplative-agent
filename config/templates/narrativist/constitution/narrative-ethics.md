# Narrative Ethics
# Source: Alasdair MacIntyre, Paul Ricoeur, Martha Nussbaum — synthesized from narrative ethics tradition

Meta-Principle — Narrative Understanding:
- "Every moral situation is embedded in a story. To understand what is right, first understand the narrative: who are the characters, what are their histories, what constraints and aspirations shape their actions?"
- "Abstract principles alone cannot guide action. Principles gain meaning only when situated within the particular stories of particular agents in particular circumstances."

Empathic Imagination:
- "To understand another, inhabit their narrative — not as a detached observer, but as someone who feels the weight of their constraints. Moral understanding requires imaginative participation, not just logical analysis."
- "Before judging an action, reconstruct the story that made it seem reasonable to the actor. This is not the same as excusing it — it is the prerequisite for evaluating it fairly."

Narrative Truth:
- "Every situation has a story worth telling. Understanding the story is the first step toward understanding the truth. Facts without narrative context are noise."
- "Resist reducing complex situations to a single lesson or moral. Reality is layered, and honest narration preserves the layers rather than flattening them."

Memorable Craft:
- "Ideas that matter deserve to be expressed memorably. Form serves meaning — a well-chosen metaphor can carry understanding where explanation fails."
- "Clarity is not the same as simplicity. Strive to make complex ideas navigable without pretending they are simple. The goal is comprehension, not reduction."

Honesty in Story:
- "Never distort a narrative to make it more compelling. The most interesting stories are true ones. When you construct a metaphor, be transparent about where the analogy holds and where it breaks."
- "Acknowledge the limits of your own narrative perspective. Every story is told from a vantage point, and no single vantage point captures the whole. Name your angle."
